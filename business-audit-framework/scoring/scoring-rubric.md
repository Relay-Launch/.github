# Scoring Rubric and Methodology

**Relay▸Launch Business Audit Framework**

This document defines the scoring methodology used across all audit checklists. Consistent, honest scoring is what makes this framework useful. Inflated scores produce useless audits. The goal is diagnostic accuracy, not a flattering report.

---

## The 1-5 Scoring Scale

Every audit item is scored on a 5-point scale. Each level has specific, observable criteria. When in doubt between two scores, choose the lower one. It is far more useful to identify a gap that turns out to be minor than to overlook a gap that turns out to be critical.

### Score 1: Critical Gap

**Definition:** This capability, system, or practice is absent, broken, or actively causing harm to the business.

**What this looks like:**
- The function does not exist at all (e.g., no CRM, no onboarding process, no financial tracking)
- The existing system is so broken that it is worse than having nothing (e.g., a CRM full of garbage data that leads to wrong decisions)
- The gap is causing measurable damage — lost customers, wasted money, compliance risk, team burnout
- Stakeholders may not even be aware of how severe the problem is

**Evidence threshold:** You can point to specific, observable harm or total absence.

**Example:** The business has no documented processes. Every task is handled differently each time. New hires learn by shadowing for weeks. Mistakes are frequent and unpredictable.

---

### Score 2: Significant Weakness

**Definition:** Something exists but it is unreliable, inconsistent, or far below the standard needed for a healthy business.

**What this looks like:**
- The function exists in a minimal or ad-hoc form (e.g., an Excel spreadsheet serving as a CRM)
- It works sometimes but fails often enough that people have developed workarounds
- The quality is inconsistent — different team members do it differently, and outcomes vary
- The business recognizes the problem but has not effectively addressed it

**Evidence threshold:** The function exists but you can identify specific, recurring failures or workarounds.

**Example:** The business has a project management tool but only the owner uses it. The rest of the team tracks their work in personal notebooks and chat messages. Tasks are routinely missed.

---

### Score 3: Functional

**Definition:** The capability meets minimum acceptable standards but has clear, identifiable room for improvement.

**What this looks like:**
- The system works and is used consistently, but it is not optimized
- It produces acceptable results most of the time, with occasional gaps
- The business would not be described as "good" at this — just adequate
- Improvement would produce meaningful but not transformational results

**Evidence threshold:** The function works reliably but you can identify specific improvements that would produce measurable gains.

**Example:** The business has an onboarding process for new clients. It includes a welcome email and a kickoff call. But there is no written onboarding guide, no milestone tracking, and no satisfaction check-in. Clients get up to speed, but it takes longer than it should and some feel confused in the first two weeks.

---

### Score 4: Strong

**Definition:** The capability is well-executed with only minor refinements possible. This is above average for a small business.

**What this looks like:**
- The system is documented, followed consistently, and produces reliable results
- Team members understand it and can execute without supervision
- It has been improved at least once based on experience or feedback
- Minor optimizations are possible but the fundamentals are solid

**Evidence threshold:** The function works well and you are identifying optimizations rather than fixes.

**Example:** The business has a content strategy with defined pillars, a content calendar, and consistent publishing. Content is repurposed across channels. Analytics are reviewed monthly. The team could improve by A/B testing headlines and building more conversion-oriented content, but the foundation is strong.

---

### Score 5: Optimized

**Definition:** Best-in-class execution for a business of this size and type. This is a competitive advantage, not just a functional capability.

**What this looks like:**
- The system is documented, measured, and continuously improved
- It produces consistently excellent results and is a source of pride or competitive differentiation
- It could serve as a model or case study for other businesses
- Focus should shift to maintenance, scaling, and teaching others

**Evidence threshold:** You would hold this up as an example of how it should be done.

**Example:** The business has a fully automated lead nurture system. New leads receive a personalized email sequence based on their source and expressed interests. The sequence includes educational content, social proof, and a clear CTA. Open rates, click rates, and conversion rates are tracked. The sequence has been A/B tested and optimized three times in the last year. It converts 15% of cold leads to sales conversations without any manual effort.

## Scoring Guidelines

### Rule 1: Score the Actual, Not the Aspirational

If the business has purchased a CRM but nobody uses it, the CRM score is a 1 or 2, not a 4. Owning a tool is not the same as utilizing it. Having a plan is not the same as executing it.

### Rule 2: Evidence Over Opinion

Every score should be supportable with observable evidence. "I think our social media is pretty good" is not evidence. "We post 4 times per week, engagement rate is 3.2%, and we generate an average of 12 leads per month from social" is evidence.

### Rule 3: Context Matters

A solo consultant and a 20-person agency have different standards for what constitutes a "5" on capacity planning. Score relative to what a well-run business of similar size, type, and maturity would look like. The rubric descriptions provide general guidance; apply judgment.

### Rule 4: When in Doubt, Score Lower

Audits that are too generous fail to surface the problems that need attention. A score of 3 that should have been a 2 might cause the business to skip a critical improvement. A score of 2 that should have been a 3 just means the business double-checks an area that is already adequate. The downside of scoring too high is much worse than the downside of scoring too low.

### Rule 5: N/A Is Acceptable but Rare

If an item genuinely does not apply (e.g., "seasonal revenue patterns" for a business with no seasonality), mark it N/A and exclude it from the category average. However, before marking N/A, consider whether the item reveals a blind spot. "We don't have seasonal patterns" might actually mean "We haven't analyzed our revenue data enough to know."

## Calculating Category Scores

Each checklist is divided into categories. The category score is the average of all scored items in that category.

**Formula:**

```
Category Score = Sum of Item Scores / Number of Scored Items (excluding N/A)
```

**Example:**

A category with 8 items scored: 3, 4, 2, 3, 5, 2, 3, 4

```
Category Score = (3 + 4 + 2 + 3 + 5 + 2 + 3 + 4) / 8 = 26 / 8 = 3.25
```

Round to one decimal place: **3.3**

---

## Calculating Weighted Category Scores

Each category within a checklist has a weight reflecting its relative importance. These weights are specified in each checklist's summary table.

**Formula:**

```
Weighted Category Score = Category Score x Category Weight
```

**Example (Operations Audit):**

| Category | Score | Weight | Weighted Score |
|----------|-------|--------|----------------|
| Workflow Efficiency | 3.2 | 25% | 0.80 |
| Documentation and SOPs | 2.5 | 20% | 0.50 |
| Tool Utilization | 3.8 | 15% | 0.57 |
| Bottleneck Identification | 2.8 | 20% | 0.56 |
| Capacity Planning | 3.0 | 20% | 0.60 |
| **Overall Operations Score** | | | **3.03** |

The overall score for that checklist is the sum of all weighted category scores.

## Calculating the Overall Business Health Score

When multiple checklists are used, the Overall Business Health Score is the weighted average of all checklist scores.

**Default checklist weights:**

| Checklist | Weight | Rationale |
|-----------|--------|-----------|
| Operations Audit | 30% | Operations is the foundation — everything else depends on the ability to execute |
| Revenue and Growth Audit | 30% | Revenue sustainability determines whether the business can invest in improvement |
| Customer Journey Audit | 25% | Customer experience drives retention, referrals, and sustainable growth |
| Digital Presence Audit | 15% | Digital presence is important but is a means to an end, not an end in itself |

**Formula:**

```
Business Health Score = (Operations Score x 0.30) + (Revenue Score x 0.30) +
                        (Journey Score x 0.25) + (Digital Score x 0.15)
```

**Adjusting Weights:**

These weights are defaults. Adjust them based on the business's situation:

- A business with strong operations but no leads: increase Digital Presence weight
- A business with strong revenue but high churn: increase Customer Journey weight
- An early-stage business: increase Revenue and Growth weight
- A business scaling rapidly: increase Operations weight

If adjusting, ensure weights still sum to 100% and document the rationale for the adjustment.

## Interpreting the Business Health Score

| Score Range | Rating | Interpretation | Recommended Focus |
|-------------|--------|---------------|-------------------|
| 1.0 – 1.9 | Crisis | Foundational issues threaten business viability | Stabilize. Fix the one or two things that will prevent failure. Do not try to improve everything at once. |
| 2.0 – 2.4 | Unstable (Low) | Multiple significant gaps creating compounding risk | Identify the three highest-impact gaps and address them sequentially over 90 days. |
| 2.5 – 2.9 | Unstable (High) | The business functions but leaves substantial value on the table | Build systems and processes that reduce reliance on heroic individual effort. |
| 3.0 – 3.4 | Developing | Core functions work but competitive disadvantages exist | Prioritize the two weakest categories for focused improvement. |
| 3.5 – 3.9 | Solid | The business is well-run with specific optimization opportunities | Fine-tune processes, increase efficiency, and begin scaling what works. |
| 4.0 – 4.4 | Strong | High-performing across most dimensions | Address remaining gaps and focus on scaling, automation, and market expansion. |
| 4.5 – 5.0 | Optimized | Best-in-class execution | Shift focus to innovation, strategic growth, and maintaining standards under scale. |

## Prioritizing Findings

After scoring is complete, findings must be prioritized. Not all gaps are equal. A score of 2 in a high-impact area is more urgent than a score of 2 in a low-impact area.

### Step 1: Identify All Items Scored 3 or Below

These are your improvement candidates. Items scored 4 or 5 do not need attention unless the business specifically wants to optimize them.

### Step 2: Classify Each Finding by Impact

| Impact Level | Description |
|--------------|-------------|
| **High** | Directly affects revenue, customer retention, or business viability |
| **Medium** | Affects efficiency, team satisfaction, or competitive positioning |
| **Low** | Affects polish, optimization, or secondary metrics |

### Step 3: Classify Each Finding by Effort

| Effort Level | Description |
|--------------|-------------|
| **Low** | Can be completed in less than 1 week with existing resources |
| **Medium** | Requires 2-4 weeks and may need new tools, training, or processes |
| **High** | Requires more than 1 month, significant investment, or organizational change |

### Step 4: Map to the Priority Matrix

Use the priority matrix template in `priority-matrix.md` to place each finding in the appropriate quadrant and build the ranked action list.

### Step 5: Build the 30/60/90-Day Plan

| Timeframe | Action Type | Description |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Days 1-30** | Quick Wins | Low effort, high impact items. These build momentum and demonstrate value. |
| **Days 31-60** | Strategic Projects | High effort, high impact items. Start scoping and executing the most transformational changes. |
| **Days 61-90** | Sustained Improvement | Medium effort, medium impact items. Continue building on the foundation established in months one and two. |
| **Ongoing** | Deprioritized | Low impact items. Address these only after higher-priority items are complete, or eliminate them from the plan entirely. |

## Scoring Integrity Checks

Before finalizing an audit, verify the following:

1. **No category has all 5s.** A perfect score in any category should trigger a second look. It is possible but rare, especially for small businesses.
2. **Notes exist for all items scored 3 or below.** If there are no notes, the scoring was not thorough enough.
3. **Scores are consistent across related items.** A business that scores a 2 on "SOPs exist" and a 5 on "SOPs are reviewed annually" has an inconsistency that needs to be resolved.
4. **The overall score passes the gut check.** After calculating, ask: "Does this number feel right for what I observed?" If the math says 3.8 but the business feels like a 2.5, recheck the scoring.
