# Priority Matrix Framework

**Relay▸Launch Business Audit Framework**

The priority matrix converts a list of audit findings into a ranked action plan. Without prioritization, businesses attempt to fix everything simultaneously, achieve nothing fully, and burn out their team in the process. The matrix forces a decision: given limited time, money, and energy, what should be addressed first?

---

## The Effort vs. Impact Matrix

The matrix has two axes:

- **Impact** (vertical axis): How much will fixing this issue improve the business? Measured in terms of revenue, efficiency, customer experience, risk reduction, or strategic positioning.
- **Effort** (horizontal axis): How much time, money, and organizational energy will it take to address this finding? Includes direct costs, team hours, learning curve, and change management.

This produces four quadrants:

```
                        HIGH IMPACT
                            |
         ┌──────────────────┼──────────────────┐
         │                  │                  │
         │   QUADRANT 2     │   QUADRANT 1     │
         │   Major          │   Quick          │
         │   Projects       │   Wins           │
         │                  │                  │
         │   Plan and       │   Do these       │
         │   schedule       │   FIRST          │
         │                  │                  │
─────────┼──────────────────┼──────────────────┼─────────
HIGH     │                  │                  │  LOW
EFFORT   │   QUADRANT 4     │   QUADRANT 3     │  EFFORT
         │   Reconsider     │   Fill-Ins       │
         │                  │                  │
         │   Deprioritize   │   Do when        │
         │   or eliminate   │   capacity       │
         │                  │                  │
         │                  │                  │
         └──────────────────┼──────────────────┘
                            |
                        LOW IMPACT
```

## Quadrant Definitions

### Quadrant 1: Quick Wins (High Impact, Low Effort)

**Action:** Do these first. Schedule them within the next 30 days.

These are the findings that will move the needle with minimal investment. They build momentum, demonstrate progress, and often create the capacity or motivation needed to tackle harder problems.

**Examples:**
- Setting up a Google Business Profile (1-2 hours, significant local search visibility gain)
- Creating an email template for requesting customer reviews (30 minutes, ongoing reputation benefit)
- Implementing a standard meeting agenda template (15 minutes, immediate efficiency improvement)
- Raising prices for new clients by 15% (1 hour to update materials, immediate margin improvement)

### Quadrant 2: Major Projects (High Impact, High Effort)

**Action:** Plan and schedule these for days 31-90. They require scoping, resource allocation, and possibly phased implementation.

These are the strategic investments that transform the business. They cannot be rushed, but they also cannot be indefinitely postponed. Start planning these while executing Quick Wins.

**Examples:**
- Building a complete client onboarding system with automation (4-6 weeks)
- Implementing a CRM and migrating all client data (3-4 weeks)
- Developing a content strategy and building an initial content library (6-8 weeks)
- Documenting all core SOPs for the business (4-8 weeks depending on complexity)

### Quadrant 3: Fill-Ins (Low Impact, Low Effort)

**Action:** Do these when there is available capacity, typically after Quick Wins are complete. Do not let these distract from higher-impact work.

These are easy improvements that are worth doing but will not change the trajectory of the business. They are good tasks for slow periods, new team members learning the systems, or when a quick confidence boost is needed.

**Examples:**
- Updating the copyright year on the website footer
- Consolidating duplicate contacts in the CRM
- Cancelling unused software subscriptions
- Standardizing email signature formatting across the team

### Quadrant 4: Reconsider (Low Impact, High Effort)

**Action:** Deprioritize, delegate, or eliminate. Revisit only if impact assessment changes.

These are the traps. They feel important because they are difficult, but the actual business impact does not justify the investment. Be honest about whether these belong on the list at all.

**Examples:**
- A complete website redesign when the current site converts adequately
- Building a custom dashboard when a spreadsheet or existing tool would suffice
- Implementing a complex automation for a process that runs twice a month
- Pursuing a social media presence on a platform where the target audience is not active

## How to Build the Priority Matrix

### Step 1: List All Findings

Pull every audit item scored 3 or below from the completed checklists. Each item becomes a finding. Write it as a specific, actionable statement.

**Bad:** "Social media needs work"
**Good:** "No social media posting schedule exists; content is published sporadically with gaps of 2-3 weeks"

### Step 2: Rate Impact for Each Finding

For each finding, estimate the impact of resolving it. Use this rating scale:

| Impact Rating | Criteria |
|---------------|----------|
| **5 - Transformational** | Would directly increase revenue by 20%+, eliminate a critical business risk, or remove a bottleneck that constrains everything else |
| **4 - Significant** | Would measurably improve revenue, efficiency, or customer experience. The business would look noticeably different after this change. |
| **3 - Moderate** | Would improve a specific area meaningfully but not change the overall business trajectory |
| **2 - Minor** | Would create a small improvement. Noticeable internally but unlikely to be felt by customers or visible in financials. |
| **1 - Negligible** | Would be marginally better than status quo. Nice to have but no real consequence if skipped. |

### Step 3: Rate Effort for Each Finding

For each finding, estimate the total effort to resolve it. Consider time, cost, complexity, and organizational change required.

| Effort Rating | Criteria |
|---------------|----------|
| **1 - Trivial** | Less than 2 hours, no cost, no organizational change, one person can do it |
| **2 - Light** | 2-8 hours, minimal cost (under $200), might need one other person's input |
| **3 - Moderate** | 1-2 weeks of focused work, may require a new tool or some budget ($200-$1,000), requires coordination |
| **4 - Substantial** | 2-6 weeks, meaningful investment ($1,000-$5,000), requires multiple people, process change |
| **5 - Major** | 6+ weeks, significant investment ($5,000+), requires organizational restructuring, new hires, or fundamental process redesign |

### Step 4: Calculate Priority Score

For each finding, calculate a simple priority score:

```
Priority Score = Impact Rating / Effort Rating
```

This produces a ratio where higher numbers indicate better return on effort.

| Priority Score | Quadrant | Action |
|---------------|----------|--------|
| 2.0 and above | Quick Win (Q1) | Do first — within 30 days |
| 1.0 – 1.9 | Major Project (Q2) or Fill-In (Q3) | Schedule based on impact level |
| Below 1.0 | Reconsider (Q4) | Deprioritize or eliminate |

For items with priority scores between 1.0 and 1.9, use the impact rating to break ties:
- Impact 4 or 5: Major Project (Q2) — schedule for days 31-90
- Impact 1 or 2: Fill-In (Q3) — do when capacity allows
- Impact 3: Use judgment based on business context

### Step 5: Build the Ranked Action List

Sort all findings by priority score (highest first). This is the order in which work should be done.

## Building a 30/60/90-Day Plan from the Matrix

### Days 1-30: Quick Wins (Quadrant 1)

Execute all Q1 items. These should be completable by one or two people without significant budget. After 30 days, the business should have:
- Updated pricing capturing more value
- A claimed and optimized Google Business Profile
- An active review generation process
- A lead follow-up sequence running
- Consistent meeting documentation
- Professional, standardized email signatures

### Days 31-60: Begin Major Projects (Quadrant 2)

Start the highest-priority Q2 items. These need proper scoping before execution:
- Week 5-6: Scope and begin revenue diversification strategy
- Week 5-6: Build content calendar and begin consistent publishing
- Week 6-8: Design and document the client onboarding system

### Days 61-90: Sustain and Expand

Continue Q2 execution and begin the next tier:
- Week 9-10: Begin SOP documentation (start with the 3 most critical processes)
- Week 9-12: Evaluate, select, and begin CRM implementation
- Address Q3 Fill-In items as capacity allows

### Beyond 90 Days

Reassess. Re-score the audit items that were addressed. Priorities may have shifted. Some Q4 items may have moved to Q2 as the business's situation changed. Run a fresh prioritization if needed.

## Tips for Effective Prioritization

1. **Do not negotiate with Q4.** If the priority score says it is low-impact and high-effort, believe it. The temptation to "just do it anyway" is how businesses waste months on projects that do not move the needle.

2. **Sequence Q2 items carefully.** Some major projects depend on others. Do not start CRM implementation before documenting what data you need to track, which requires at minimum basic SOPs.

3. **Protect Quick Win momentum.** The first 30 days set the tone. If the business sees tangible results from Quick Wins, it builds confidence and commitment for the harder work ahead. Do not dilute this by starting Major Projects too early.

4. **Revisit the matrix quarterly.** Business conditions change. A finding that was low impact six months ago may be high impact now. The matrix is a living tool, not a one-time exercise.

5. **Be honest about effort estimates.** Small businesses consistently underestimate effort. If you are unsure, round up. A project estimated at 2 weeks that takes 6 weeks destroys the entire plan. A project estimated at 6 weeks that takes 4 is a pleasant surprise.
