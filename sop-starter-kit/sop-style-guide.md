# SOP Style Guide

> How to write SOPs that people actually read and follow.

---

## Tone

Write like you're explaining a process to a smart colleague who's new to the company. Be direct, clear, and specific. Avoid jargon unless your audience uses it daily.

**Do:** "Send the welcome email within 24 hours of receiving the signed contract."
**Don't:** "Ensure timely delivery of initial client communication materials post-contract execution."

---

## Formatting Conventions

### File Naming

Use lowercase kebab-case: `employee-onboarding.md`, `monthly-financial-close.md`

### Document Structure

Every SOP should include these sections in this order:

1. **Title** — Clear, descriptive name
2. **Metadata table** — SOP number, version, dates, owner, department
3. **Purpose** — Why this SOP exists (1-2 sentences)
4. **Scope** — Who and what this applies to
5. **Roles & Responsibilities** — Who does what
6. **Prerequisites** — What must be in place before starting
7. **Procedure** — The actual steps
8. **Exceptions** — When to deviate from the standard process
9. **Related Documents** — Links to other relevant SOPs or resources
10. **Revision History** — What changed and when

### Numbering Convention

Format: `SOP-[DEPT]-[NUMBER]`

| Prefix | Department |
|---|---|
| OPS | Operations |
| FIN | Finance |
| HR | Human Resources |
| SAL | Sales |
| MKT | Marketing |
| IT | Information Technology |
| CS | Customer Service |

Examples: `SOP-OPS-001`, `SOP-FIN-003`, `SOP-HR-012`

### Writing Steps

- Use numbered lists for sequential steps (order matters)
- Use bullet lists for non-sequential items (order doesn't matter)
- Use checkboxes (`- [ ]`) for items that need to be confirmed or completed
- Start each step with a verb: "Send", "Review", "Update", "Create", "Verify"
- One action per step — if a step has "and" in it, consider splitting it
- Include the expected timeframe when relevant ("within 24 hours", "by end of day")

### Formatting Text

- **Bold** for role names, tool names, and critical actions
- *Italics* for notes, clarifications, or optional context
- `Code formatting` for specific field names, system values, or file paths
- Use tables for structured reference information
- Use horizontal rules (`---`) to separate major sections

---

## Versioning

- Start every new SOP at version **1.0**
- **Minor updates** (typo fixes, clarifications, adding a note): increment to 1.1, 1.2, etc.
- **Major updates** (new steps, changed process, different tools): increment to 2.0, 3.0, etc.
- Always update the revision history table when making changes
- Never delete old revision history entries

---

## Review Cadence

| SOP Type | Review Frequency |
|---|---|
| Core operations (daily processes) | Every 6 months |
| Supporting processes (weekly/monthly) | Annually |
| Compliance / regulatory | As regulations change, minimum annually |
| New SOPs | After 30 days, then standard cadence |

Set calendar reminders for reviews. An outdated SOP is worse than no SOP because people follow the wrong instructions.

---

## Writing Tips

1. **Walk the process first.** Don't write from memory. Actually do the process (or watch someone do it) and write down every step as it happens.

2. **Include the why.** When a step isn't obvious, a brief explanation prevents people from skipping it. "Lock the accounting period (prevents accidental edits to closed months)."

3. **Name the tools.** Don't say "update the system." Say "update the status in HubSpot."

4. **Be specific about timing.** "Respond quickly" means different things to different people. "Respond within 4 business hours" is clear.

5. **Test with a new person.** Have someone who doesn't know the process try to follow the SOP. If they get stuck or confused, the SOP needs work.

6. **Keep it maintainable.** The best SOP is one that stays current. Don't over-document — write what's needed to execute correctly, nothing more.
